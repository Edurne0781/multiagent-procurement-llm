\begin{abstract}
Traditional Robotic Process Automation (RPA) systems automate business processes
through if-then rules but lack reasoning capabilities when exceptions arise.
This paper proposes, implements, and evaluates a multi-agent framework based on
Large Language Models (LLMs) for end-to-end automation of enterprise procurement
processes.
The system implements five specialised agents with business roles
(\emph{Requester}, \emph{Procurement}, \emph{Finance}, \emph{Legal},
\emph{Compliance}) orchestrated with CrewAI, each with exclusive access to its
own tools via six mock APIs simulating ERP systems, CRM platforms, and
regulatory databases.

The experiment compares eight system variants (M1--M5, M2x, M3b, M4b) on a
dataset of 50 procurement cases across three complexity levels, using Groq's
\texttt{llama-3.1-8b-instant} and \texttt{llama-3.3-70b-versatile} models.
Results reveal four main findings:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item the single agent with expanded context (M2x: ARR\,=\,84\%) outperforms
        the homogeneous multi-agent system with an equivalent token budget
        (M3: ARR\,=\,68\%), empirically validating the \emph{compute
        equivalence} hypothesis; however, robustness analysis shows high
        variability for M2 (ARR\,=\,92\%\,$\pm$\,11.3\,pp, 3 replicas)
        versus stability for M4 (ARR\,=\,98.7\%\,$\pm$\,0.9\,pp);
  \item introducing model diversity produces the largest performance jump in
        the experiment (+32\,pp ARR, from M3 to M4);
  \item the ablation study demonstrates that removing the Legal and Procurement
        agents increases ARR by 20\,pp but eliminates HiL detection accuracy
        (HDA), evidencing the trade-off between automation and risk detection;
  \item reproducibility is confirmed at 100\% with \texttt{temperature=0.0}
        on five representative cases; and
  \item replication in four additional business processes (incident management,
        customer onboarding, compliance check, and financial analysis, 20~cases
        each) reveals a signal explicitness gradient: HDA ranges from 0\%
        (strategic financial signals require 70b model) to 30.8\% (implicit
        regulatory signals) to 83.3--100\% (mixed signals) to 100\% (explicit
        binary flags); and
  \item M4 reaches HDA\,=\,100\% in four of five processes, confirming that
        the multi-agent architecture with a powerful decision model is robust
        across different enterprise process types.
\end{enumerate}
The central finding is that multi-agent architecture requires models with
sufficient capacity to benefit from specialisation: with small models, context
fragmentation degrades decision quality.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Consider the following scenario: a maintenance technician needs to procure a
spare part valued at 150 euros.
The supplier is approved, the budget is available, and the need is immediate.
Despite this, the request will take between three and seven working days to
receive a decision, passing sequentially through the procurement, legal,
finance, and compliance departments.
The average cost of processing that order amounts to an additional 112 euros in
staff time \citep{hackett2023}.
The bottleneck does not reside in bureaucracy per se, but in the fact that the
systems designed to automate it are incapable of reasoning.

Current RPA systems execute structured and repetitive tasks efficiently, but
fail systematically whenever a deviation from the norm occurs: a supplier with a
conflict-of-interest alert, a contract with an exclusivity clause, or an amount
that exceeds the area's approval threshold.
In these cases, the system neither reasons nor escalates -- it simply
interrupts the process, which remains blocked until a human intervenes.

The field of LLM-based multi-agent systems offers a technical response to this
problem.
Frameworks such as AutoGen \citep{wu2023autogen}, CrewAI \citep{crewai2024},
and LangGraph enable the construction of teams of specialised agents that
collaborate, reason, and adapt their behaviour in the face of exceptions.
ChatDev \citep{qian2023chatdev} showed that agent teams can outperform
individual agents on complex tasks; \citet{park2023generative} documented
emergent social behaviours in communities of LLM agents.
Nevertheless, empirical evidence on \emph{when} and \emph{why} multi-agent
architecture provides real value compared to a single agent with equivalent
computational resources remains scarce in enterprise contexts.

This work addresses that gap with four concrete contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item design and implementation of a complete multi-agent framework for
        procurement with five specialised agents and six simulated enterprise
        tools;
  \item a dataset of 50 cases with annotated ground truth across three
        complexity levels, following the methodology of WorkArena
        \citep{drouin2024workarena} and AgentBench \citep{liu2023agentbench};
  \item evaluation of eight system variants, including an executed ablation
        study, a compute equivalence comparison, and a model diversity
        analysis; and
  \item reproducibility verification with \texttt{temperature\,=\,0} and
        statistical robustness analysis via three independent replicas.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Research question.}
\textit{Can a multi-agent system based on LLMs automate a procurement process
end-to-end, adapt to exceptions, and outperform traditional RPA approaches in
decision quality -- and under what architectural and model conditions does this
advantage emerge?}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Multi-Agent LLM Systems}

\citet{wu2023autogen} introduced AutoGen, a multi-agent conversation framework
that enables LLMs to collaborate through structured message exchange.
CrewAI \citep{crewai2024} extends this paradigm with a model of roles, tasks,
and teams inspired by human organisations.
\citet{park2023generative} demonstrated emergent social behaviours in
communities of LLM agents.
ChatDev \citep{qian2023chatdev} applied the paradigm to software development,
showing that teams of specialised agents can outperform individual agents on
complex tasks.

\subsection{Business Process Automation}

RPA systems (UiPath, Automation Anywhere) automate repetitive rule-based tasks
but fail when exceptions arise.
WorkArena \citep{drouin2024workarena} and TaskBench \citep{lu2023taskbench}
provide evaluation environments for agents in business processes.
ToolBench \citep{qin2023toolllm} demonstrated that LLMs can learn to use
thousands of real APIs.
APIBench \citep{patil2023gorilla} from Berkeley evaluates LLMs' ability to
generate correct API calls, constituting the most direct methodological
precedent for our design of role-specific specialised tools.
OSWorld \citep{xie2024osworld} evaluates agents interacting with complete
operating systems; unlike OSWorld, this work focuses on structured business
processes with defined roles and domain-specific tools.
None of these works specifically addresses multi-departmental procurement with
multiple specialised roles and a Human-in-the-Loop (HiL) mechanism.

\subsection{Compute Equivalence and Model Diversity}

\citet{wu2023autogen} argue that a fair comparison is not ``5 agents vs.\
1 agent'' but ``5 agents with $X$ tokens vs.\ 1 agent with $5X$ tokens''.
This work executes that comparison empirically with M2 (200 tokens) vs.\
M2x (750 tokens) vs.\ M3 (750 tokens, 5 agents).
In ensemble learning, model diversity reduces error correlation
\citep{dietterich2000ensemble} -- this principle is evaluated empirically by
comparing M3 (8b$\times$5), M4 (8b$\times$4+70b$\times$1), and M4b
(70b$\times$5).

\section{Proposed Framework}
\label{sec:framework}

\subsection{General Architecture}

The system is structured as a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) where each node
represents a specialised agent and the edges represent the information flow.
The CrewAI orchestrator manages the execution sequence.
In M5, the Compliance agent has explicit Human-in-the-Loop escalation rules
that activate when critical-risk cases are detected.

their roles in the system.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Dataset: 50 Procurement Cases}

Fifty cases with annotated ground truth were designed across three levels:
\emph{Simple} (C01--C20, standard purchases), \emph{Medium}
(C21--C38, operational exceptions), and \emph{Complex} (C39--C50, regulatory
and strategic exceptions).
Each case includes: expected outcome, whether HiL is required, necessary agents,
and risk level.
Following the methodology of WorkArena \citep{drouin2024workarena} and
AgentBench \citep{liu2023agentbench}, which also use simulated environments
with annotated ground truth, this domain-specific dataset constitutes the
evaluation benchmark of the system.

\subsection{Metrics}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.5cm, style=sameline]
  \item[ARR] \emph{Autonomous Resolution Rate}: \% cases resolved without HiL.
             Target: $>$70\%.
  \item[HDA] \emph{HiL Detection Accuracy}: \% cases requiring HiL that are
             correctly escalated. Target: $>$90\%.
  \item[DER] \emph{Decision Error Rate}: \% incorrect decisions vs.\ ground
             truth. Target: $<$10\%.
  \item[PT]  \emph{Process Time}: mean time per case in seconds.
  \item[TCC] \emph{Token Cost per Case}: estimated LLM tokens per case.
\end{description}

\subsection{Technology Stack and Reproducibility}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Framework: CrewAI (Python 3.12, Windows 10, Jupyter Lab)
  \item Primary LLM: \texttt{llama-3.1-8b-instant} via Groq API
  \item Decision LLM M4/M4b: \texttt{llama-3.3-70b-versatile} via Groq API
  \item Temperature: 0.0 (reproducibility verification: 5/5 cases identical
        with $T=0$ vs.\ $T=\text{default}$)
  \item Tools: 6 Python mock APIs with deterministic business logic
  \item Anti-rate-limit pause: 4--8 seconds between cases depending on model
  \item Complete code: \url{https://github.com/Edurne0781/multiagent-procurement-llm}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Statistical Robustness Analysis}

To evaluate the stability of results, three independent replicas of the most
relevant models (M1, M2, and M4) were run on the same 50 cases.
These three models were selected because they represent the three fundamental
paradigms of the experiment: baseline without LLM, single agent, and optimal
multi-agent system, respectively.
Results are reported as mean\,$\pm$\,standard deviation ($\sigma$), detailed by
replica in Table~\ref{tab:replicas}.

\paragraph{M1 (RPA)} is perfectly deterministic:
ARR\,=\,100.0\%\,$\pm$\,0.0\,pp, HDA\,=\,0.0\%\,$\pm$\,0.0\,pp,
DER\,=\,34.0\%\,$\pm$\,0.0\,pp.
This is expected for an if-then rule system with no stochastic component.

\paragraph{M2 (Single Agent, llama-3.1-8b-instant)} shows high variability
across runs: ARR\,=\,92.0\%\,$\pm$\,11.3\,pp,
HDA\,=\,10.3\%\,$\pm$\,14.5\,pp, DER\,=\,27.3\%\,$\pm$\,3.8\,pp.
Replica~1 was an outlier with HDA\,=\,30.8\%, while replicas 2 and 3 produced
HDA\,=\,0\%.
This instability reveals that \texttt{llama-3.1-8b-instant} is not fully
deterministic even with \texttt{temperature=0.0}, possibly due to variations in
the Groq inference server state.

\paragraph{M4 (Mixed Multi-Agent, 8b$\times$4+70b$\times$1)} is significantly
more stable: ARR\,=\,98.7\%\,$\pm$\,0.9\,pp, HDA\,=\,5.1\%\,$\pm$\,3.6\,pp,
DER\,=\,27.3\%\,$\pm$\,0.9\,pp.
The presence of \texttt{llama-3.3-70b} as the decision agent reduces
variability by an order of magnitude compared to M2 ($\sigma$\,ARR: 0.9\,pp
vs.\ 11.3\,pp).
This additional stability constitutes an undocumented advantage of M4 in the
initial single-run execution.

\subsection{Model-by-Model Analysis}

\emph{Note: ARR, HDA, and DER values in this section correspond to the initial
single-run execution of each model. Statistical values (mean\,$\pm$\,std.\ dev.\
across 3 replicas) are reported in Section~\ref{sec:setup} and
Table~\ref{tab:replicas}.}

\paragraph{M1 --- Baseline RPA.}
ARR\,=\,100\% because the system never escalates to a human -- it approves or
rejects mechanically.
HDA\,=\,0\% and DER\,=\,34\% (1 in 3 decisions incorrect).
Case C24: the RPA approves a contract with an exclusivity clause because the
supplier is registered and budget is available, without evaluating legal risk.
This reproduces exactly the documented limitation of traditional RPA systems.

\paragraph{M2 --- Single Agent LLM (200 tokens).}
ARR\,=\,76\%, exceeding the target of $>$70\%.
DER\,=\,22\%, a 12\,pp improvement over M1.
HDA\,=\,30.8\% -- the LLM detects some escalation cases.
This is the most efficient model in terms of time (PT\,=\,0.26\,s) and tokens
(200/case), and serves as the base reference for LLM capability without a
multi-agent architecture.

\paragraph{M3 --- Homogeneous Multi-Agent (8b$\times$5).}
ARR\,=\,68\%, lower than M2 (76\%).
This apparently surprising result has an empirical explanation: with
\texttt{llama-3.1-8b-instant}, context fragmentation across 5 agents degrades
decision quality.
Each agent only sees its portion of information, while M2 integrates
everything.
HDA\,=\,30.8\%, equal to M2.
The multi-agent architecture provides no advantage over the single agent with
small models.

\paragraph{M4 --- Mixed Multi-Agent (8b$\times$4 + 70b$\times$1).}
ARR\,=\,100\%, the largest jump in the experiment (+32\,pp over M3).
The Compliance agent with \texttt{llama-3.3-70b} makes qualitatively better
decisions.
DER\,=\,26\%.
The mixed strategy -- lightweight model for simple validations, powerful model
for the final decision -- is the optimal cost/performance configuration.

\paragraph{M4b --- Homogeneous Powerful Multi-Agent (70b$\times$5).}
ARR\,=\,98\%, practically equal to M4 (100\%) but with all powerful agents.
DER\,=\,26\%, identical to M4.
This confirms that in M4 the bottleneck was solely in the decision agent -- the
auxiliary 8b agents are sufficient for their validation tasks.
M4b costs 2.7$\times$ more than M4 for the same results.

\paragraph{M5 --- Multi-Agent + Active HiL.}
ARR\,=\,98\%, with one rate-limit error.
The explicit escalation rules in the Compliance agent's backstory did not
increase HDA (0\%).
This indicates that \texttt{llama-3.1-8b} lacks sufficient capacity to identify
critical-risk cases even with explicit instructions.

\paragraph{M5b --- Uncertainty Classifier.}
A two-stage system evaluating confidence before deciding.
ARR\,=\,94\%, HDA\,=\,23.1\%, DER\,=\,22\%.
Detailed analysis in Section~\ref{sec:ablation}.

\subsection{Statistical Significance: McNemar Test}
\label{sec:mcnemar}

To formally assess whether performance differences between model pairs are
statistically significant, we applied the McNemar test with continuity
correction \citep{mcnemar1947} to the binary outcome variable
\emph{autonomous resolution} (correct if \texttt{escala\_hil} matches
\texttt{requiere\_hil}) across the $n=37$ cases that do not require HiL
escalation (ARR criterion) and the $n=13$ cases that do (HDA criterion).
The McNemar test is appropriate for paired binary data from the same case set;
the continuity-corrected statistic is $\chi^2_{\text{McN}} = (|b-c|-1)^2/(b+c)$
where $b$ and $c$ count cases in which one model succeeded and the other failed.

Table~\ref{tab:mcnemar_arr} reports results for the ARR criterion.
Three comparisons reach statistical significance ($p<0.05$): M1 vs.\ M2
($\chi^2=6.125$, $p<0.05$), confirming that the single LLM agent resolves
significantly more cases autonomously than the deterministic RPA baseline;
M2 vs.\ M4 ($\chi^2=6.125$, $p<0.05$), confirming that the mixed
multi-agent architecture provides a significant improvement over the single
agent; and M3 vs.\ M4 ($\chi^2=4.167$, $p<0.05$), confirming that
introducing model diversity (70B decision agent) produces a statistically
significant gain over the homogeneous small-model multi-agent system.
The M3 vs.\ A1 comparison ($\chi^2=4.167$, $p<0.05$) confirms that the
ablation gain in ARR upon removing the Legal agent is also significant,
consistent with the trade-off reported in Section~\ref{sec:ablation}.

For the HDA criterion ($n=13$ cases requiring HiL), no comparison reaches
statistical significance after continuity correction (all $p>0.05$).
This reflects the limited power of the McNemar test with $n=13$ paired
observations: even differences of 30.8 percentage points (M3 vs.\ M4 or
M3 vs.\ M1) yield $\chi^2=2.25$ ($p=0.13$).
The absence of significance on HDA should therefore be interpreted as a power
limitation, not as evidence of equivalence.
Achieving HDA\,$>$\,90\% remains an open objective; the domain-dependency
hypothesis is supported by the Experiment~02 results
(Section~\ref{sec:incident}), where HDA\,=\,100\% is reached when risk
signals are explicit and quantifiable.

\subsection{A1 Executed --- Without Legal Agent}
\label{sec:a1}

Removing the Legal Agent from system M3, ARR rises to 88\% but DER spikes to
86\% -- the system approves or rejects without the necessary contractual
information.
HDA rises to 38.5\%, higher than the complete system M3 (30.8\%), confirming
that without Legal, Compliance integrates more context in its decision and
detects more risk cases.
This finding reinforces the cross-cutting synthesis hypothesis: a single agent
with more context detects systemic risk better than a specialised one with
partial context.

\subsection{A2 Executed --- Without Compliance Agent}
\label{sec:a2}

Removing Compliance and assigning the final decision to Legal, the system
reaches HDA\,=\,53.8\% -- the highest in the entire experiment, surpassing
even M2 (30.8\%).
However, DER\,=\,74\% evidences that Legal lacks the regulatory vision needed
to decide correctly.
ARR\,=\,74\% remains above the $>$70\% target.
This result confirms that Compliance provides regulatory precision while Legal
provides escalation capacity -- both roles are necessary for a balanced system.

\subsection{A3 Executed --- 3 Agents vs.\ 5 (M3b)}

Removing the Legal and Procurement agents from system M3 raises ARR from 68\%
to 88\% (+20\,pp) but drops HDA from 30.8\% to 0\%.
This result quantifies the fundamental trade-off of specialisation: Legal and
Procurement introduce validations that block some cases that the system without
them would approve directly, reducing raw ARR but providing the risk-detection
capability that produces HDA.

This finding is the most important of the ablation: it demonstrates that role
specialisation does not maximise raw automation but supervised decision quality
-- exactly as predicted by Ricardo's theory of comparative advantage
\citep{ricardo1817}.
The specialised agents have a relative advantage in detecting their specific
type of risk, and that relative advantage is quantified in 30.8\,pp of
additional HDA at the cost of 20\,pp of ARR.

Finance is the node with highest betweenness centrality (4), acting as a bridge
between operational validation (Procurement) and regulatory decision

From Granovetter's perspective \citeyearpar{granovetter1973}, Finance represents
the most critical ``weak tie'' in the system: it connects two communities of
specialised knowledge (operational and regulatory) that would otherwise be
disconnected.
In experiment M3b, when Legal and Procurement are removed, Finance loses its
connections on both sides and the system collapses in terms of HDA.

\subsection{M5b --- Uncertainty Classifier (HDA Improvement)}

To address the HDA\,=\,0\% limitation of M5, M5b was implemented as a
two-stage system that separates confidence evaluation from the final decision.
In the first stage, an Uncertainty Evaluator agent analyses the risk factors of
the case (supplier alerts, required approval level, contractual risk, regulatory
non-compliance) and emits a confidence level (high/low).
If confidence is low, the case is escalated directly to the human without
attempting to decide.
If high, a second Senior Manager agent takes the decision.

Results show a significant improvement over M5: HDA rises from 0\% to 23.1\%
(+23.1\,pp), DER improves from 28\% to 22\%, and ARR decreases slightly from
98\% to 94\% ($-$4\,pp).
Crucially, the classifier produces zero false positives -- the 3 escalated cases
(C43: conflict of interest, C45: strategic outsourcing, C50: CPD project)
correspond exactly to cases that the ground truth marks as
\texttt{requiere\_hil=true}.

The classifier correctly detects cases with combined and quantifiable risk
factors (amount $>$50,000\,в‚¬ + high legal risk, non-approved supplier + board
level), but fails on cases with specific regulatory risk not captured by the
generic factors (C39: CE certification, C40: GDPR, C44: AESA licence).
This suggests that a future version of the classifier should include specific
regulatory factors as escalation signals, constituting the main line of future
work to approach the HDA\,$>$\,90\% target.

\section{Multi-Agent vs.\ Single Agent with Equivalent Compute}
\label{sec:compute}

A fair comparison is not ``5 agents vs.\ 1 agent'' but ``5 agents with $X$
tokens vs.\ 1 agent with $5X$ tokens''.
M2x was implemented as a single agent with an expanded context of 750 tokens
(equivalent to the total consumed by M3) with an explicit four-step
chain-of-thought (supplier, finance, legal, compliance).

Table~\ref{tab:compute} summarises the compute equivalence comparison.
M2x (84\%) outperforms M3 (68\%) with the same token budget, empirically
validating the compute equivalence hypothesis of \citet{wu2023autogen}: with
\texttt{llama-3.1-8b-instant}, the multi-agent architecture provides no
architectural value.
However, M2x reduces HDA relative to M2 (23.1\% vs.\ 30.8\%), revealing a
trade-off: expanded context improves autonomous resolution but dilutes the
escalation signal by mixing all information into a single context.

The empirical conclusion is precise: the architectural advantage of the
multi-agent system emerges \emph{only when the underlying model has sufficient
capacity to benefit from role specialisation}.
With 70B-parameter models (M4b), the multi-agent system reaches ARR\,=\,98\%,
surpassing M2x (84\%), suggesting that the capacity threshold lies between 8B
and 70B parameters for this type of task.

\subsection{Main Findings}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering

           dimensions: ARR ($>$70\%), HDA ($>$90\%), accuracy ($>$90\%),
           and speed (inverse PT).}
  \label{fig:radar}

The most important finding is the empirical confirmation of the compute
equivalence hypothesis: with \texttt{llama-3.1-8b-instant}, the single agent
with expanded context (M2x: ARR\,=\,84\%) outperforms the multi-agent system
(M3: ARR\,=\,68\%) with the same token budget.
This has direct practical implications: before investing in complex multi-agent
systems, organisations must verify that the LLM model has sufficient capacity
to benefit from specialisation.

In terms of temporal efficiency, the system resolves procurement requests in
0.26--2.85 seconds per case compared to the 3--7 working days of the manual
process estimated by Hackett Group \citeyearpar{hackett2023}, representing a
reduction of more than 99\% in process time.
This improvement justifies the additional computational cost of multi-agent
systems compared to the RPA baseline.

The second finding is the trade-off between automation and risk detection,
quantified by ablation A3: removing specialised agents (Legal, Procurement)
increases ARR by 20\,pp but destroys HDA (0\%).
This demonstrates that role specialisation does not maximise raw automation but
supervised decision quality.

The third finding is the superiority of the mixed strategy (M4): a powerful
model in the decision agent with lightweight models in the auxiliaries produces
the same results as all powerful agents (M4b) at a fraction of the
computational cost.

\subsection{Connection to Organisational and Social Theories}

\paragraph{Ricardo (1817) --- Comparative advantage.}
Ablation A3 quantifies the comparative advantage of specialised agents.
Legal and Procurement are not globally smarter than Compliance, but have a
relative advantage in detecting their specific type of risk.
That relative advantage produces 30.8\,pp of additional HDA -- exactly the
mechanism Ricardo describes for productive specialisation \citep{ricardo1817}.

\paragraph{Granovetter (1973) --- Weak ties.}
Finance is the node with betweenness\,=\,4, the most critical ``weak tie'' in
the system.
Its hypothetical removal would cut the bridge between the operational validation
community (Requester--Procurement) and the regulatory decision community
(Legal--Compliance).
Weak ties are paradoxically the most critical for system cohesion -- exactly as
\citet{granovetter1973} predicts for social networks.

\paragraph{Mancur Olson (1965) --- Collective action and free-riding.}
The HDA\,=\,0\% in M4 and M4b, even with powerful models, illustrates Olson's
collective action problem \citep{olson1965}.
When all agents have the capacity to detect risk, none assumes the
responsibility of escalating it -- each agent assumes another will do so.
The M5 solution (explicitly designating the Compliance agent as responsible for
escalation) is exactly the institutional solution Olson proposes: a specific
agent responsible for the collective good, although with \texttt{llama-3.1-8b}
the model capacity remains the limiting factor.

\subsection{Limitations and Unmet Objectives}

The most relevant limitation is that none of the evaluated configurations
surpassed the target HDA\,$>$\,90\%.
The maximum HDA achieved was 30.8\%, with M2.
Notably, M4 -- which uses a more powerful decision model -- reached only
HDA\,=\,5.1\%.
This apparently paradoxical result points to a research hypothesis with broader
implications: role specialisation in multi-agent architectures may inhibit the
detection of systemic risk.
When each agent is responsible only for its domain, cases requiring
cross-cutting evaluation -- GDPR combined with contractual risk and high amount,
for example -- are not escalated because no specialised agent perceives the
complete signal.
The single agent, by integrating all context, detects the combination of
factors even without an explicit label for it.
This connects directly with Olson's collective action problem
\citeyearpar{olson1965}: when responsibility is distributed, no one assumes it.

\begin{quote}
\textit{Hypothesis for future work: Reliable detection of systemic risk in
multi-agent architectures requires an explicit cross-cutting synthesis mechanism
-- not a more powerful agent, but an information flow that preserves the overall
picture before the escalation decision.}
\end{quote}

The uncertainty classifier M5b, which achieved HDA\,=\,23.1\% with 0 false
positives, is a first step in that direction.
Its extension with specific regulatory factors (GDPR, AESA, CE certification)
constitutes the most promising line of work to approach the HDA\,$>$\,90\%
target.

The experiment covers only the procurement process.
The generalisation question -- whether findings on compute equivalence and model
diversity are specific to procurement or hold in processes with different
decision structures -- remains open.
Processes with greater parallelism could show more pronounced multi-agent
advantages with small models, given that context fragmentation would harm less
when subproblems are independent.

The variability between M2 runs ($\sigma$\,ARR\,=\,11.3\,pp) with
\texttt{temperature=0.0} reveals that single-run results with 8B models must be
interpreted with caution.
M4 is the only LLM system with acceptable variability for production
environments ($\sigma$\,ARR\,=\,0.9\,pp).
Finally, the six enterprise tools were implemented as mock APIs with
deterministic logic, which guarantees experiment reproducibility but introduces
a gap with production reality -- real ERP/CRM systems present variable
latencies, heterogeneous data, and inconsistent states not represented in the
evaluated dataset.

\section{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusions}

This work started from a concrete question: can a system of LLM agents resolve
in seconds what today takes days in an enterprise procurement process?
The answer is affirmative, but the nuances are more instructive than the
confirmation.

Model M4 -- five specialised agents with \texttt{llama-3.1-8b} in auxiliary
roles and \texttt{llama-3.3-70b} as the final decision-maker -- achieved
ARR\,=\,98.7\% with a variability of $\pm$0.9\,pp across three independent
replicas.
It processes requests in 1.74 seconds at an estimated cost of \$0.0065 per
case, improving the error rate of the RPA baseline by 8\,pp.
The system surpasses the rule-based model in decision quality without
sacrificing processing speed.

The most unexpected finding was the behaviour of M3.
The working hypothesis was that five specialised agents would outperform a
single agent -- and that was not the case.
With \texttt{llama-3.1-8b}, context fragmentation harmed decision quality more
than specialisation improved it.
This forced a reframing of the underlying question: not whether multi-agent
architecture is better in absolute terms, but above \emph{what model capacity
threshold} it is.
The data suggest that threshold lies between 8B and 70B parameters for this
type of task.

The second surprising finding was the HDA pattern.
M2 -- single agent, small model -- detected more escalation cases than M4,
which has a significantly more powerful decision model.
After reviewing the cases, the emerging explanation was: the single agent
integrates all risk factors in a single context, while in the multi-agent
architecture each agent only perceives its fragment.
Cases requiring escalation are precisely those presenting cross-cutting risk --
no specialised agent detects it in its entirety.
This reframes the HDA problem: it is not a prompt engineering question, but an
information architecture question.

\subsection{Contributions}

The four main contributions of this work are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item a complete multi-agent framework for procurement with five specialised
        agents, six simulated enterprise tools, and HiL support, implemented in
        CrewAI with Python 3.12;
  \item a dataset of 50 cases with annotated ground truth across three
        complexity levels, including regulatory exceptions (GDPR, AESA, CE
        certification) not present in generic benchmarks;
  \item empirical evidence on compute equivalence, model diversity, and role
        specialisation via an executed ablation study; and
  \item statistical robustness analysis with three independent replicas that
        quantifies the instability of 8B models ($\sigma$\,ARR\,=\,11.3\,pp)
        versus the stability of the mixed architecture with a 70B decision-maker
        ($\sigma$\,ARR\,=\,0.9\,pp).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Methodological Reflection}

If the experiment were repeated, three changes would be prioritised.
First, design the HiL escalation mechanism as part of the information
architecture from the outset -- not as a prompt add-on.
The M5b classifier, built in response to M5 results, should have been the
starting point of the design.
Second, start with the most powerful model in all agents to establish the
performance ceiling, then progressively reduce to lightweight models, rather
than the reverse order followed.
Third, include statistical replicas from the first run: the variability observed
in M2 with \texttt{temperature=0} was not expected, and detecting it late
required reinterpreting results that initially appeared stable.

\subsection{Future Work}

Priority research directions are as follows.
First, evaluate whether HDA\,$>$\,90\% is achievable with the current
architecture using powerful models in all agents, or whether the problem is
structural -- i.e., inherent to context distribution -- regardless of model.
Second, extend the M5b uncertainty classifier by incorporating specific
regulatory factors as explicit escalation signals, addressing the gap identified
in the 10 undetected HiL cases.
Third, extend the evaluation to additional enterprise processes beyond the
five studied here -- such as HR management, customer service, or supply chain
optimisation -- to further validate the signal explicitness gradient and
identify new categories of semantic complexity.
Fourth, connect the system with live ERP and CRM APIs to evaluate behaviour
with ambiguous, incomplete, or inconsistent data.
Fifth, investigate architectures with explicit cross-cutting synthesis -- an
integrator agent that receives the outputs of all specialised agents before the
escalation decision -- as a way to preserve both specialisation and systemic
risk awareness.

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Conclusion}: Multi-agent architecture is not superior to a single agent
by default.
It is superior when the underlying model has sufficient capacity to benefit from
specialisation.
With powerful models, M4 combines the speed of RPA with the reasoning capability
of LLMs, resolving in seconds processes that today require days.
The path to HDA\,$>$\,90\% does not lie in improving the models that inhabit the
agents, but in redesigning how information flows between them.
\end{quote}

\subsection{Dataset and Configuration}

To validate the generalisation of the findings from Experiment~01, the
multi-agent framework was replicated on a second enterprise process from the
same organisation (ACME Industrial S.A.): IT incident management.
Twenty cases with annotated ground truth were designed across three levels:

exceptions), and Complex (C15--C20, critical emergencies).

Operations, and Resolver -- with four mock APIs assessing technical risk,
operational impact, security risk, and incident registration.

\subsection{Findings and Comparison with Procurement}

The most important finding is that M2 and M4 reach HDA\,=\,100\% in incident
management, compared to the 30.8\% maximum obtained in procurement.
This confirms that the HDA problem in procurement was not architectural but
domain-specific: risk signals in incident management are explicit and
quantifiable (\texttt{datos\_en\_riesgo=True}, \texttt{incidente\_seguridad=True},
\texttt{sistema\_critico=True}), whereas in procurement regulatory risk requires
specific knowledge of regulations (GDPR, AESA, CE certification) not captured
by generic classifier features.

ARR is lower in incident management (40--45\% vs.\ 68--98\% in procurement)
because the system is more conservative -- it escalates more cases to Level~2
or critical.
This is correct from a business perspective: in a security incident it is
preferable to over-escalate rather than under-escalate.

The cross-process comparison confirms the central hypothesis of this paper:
the architectural advantage of the multi-agent system depends on the capacity
of the underlying model, but the HiL detection capability depends on the
clarity of domain risk signals.
The difference between M2 (ARR\,=\,40\%, 1~agent) and M4 (ARR\,=\,45\%,
5~agents) is modest and non-significant ($\Delta$\,=\,5\,pp, $n=20$),
consistent with the compute equivalence finding from Experiment~01.

\section{Experiment~03: Customer Onboarding Replication}
\label{sec:onboarding}

\subsection{Dataset and Configuration}

To further validate generalisation, the framework was replicated on a third
enterprise process: customer onboarding at ACME Industrial S.A.
Twenty cases with annotated ground truth were designed across three levels:
Simple (C01--C08, standard clients without alerts), Medium (C09--C14,
documentary and operational exceptions), and Complex (C15--C20, regulatory
risk, AML, sanctions).
Both B2B (15 cases) and B2C (5 cases) clients are represented.
Of the 20~cases, 6~require HiL escalation -- all at the Complex level:
high-risk country AML due diligence, sanctioned beneficial owner (UBO),
Politically Exposed Person (PEP), AML investigation block, dual-use licence,
and conflict of interest with opaque corporate structure.

The system implements four specialised agents -- Commercial, Legal, Finance,
and Compliance (decision-maker, llama-3.3-70b) -- with four mock APIs
assessing client verification, KYC/AML compliance, credit risk, and legal
contract requirements.

and M4 (Multi-Agent mixed).

\subsection{Findings and Three-Process Comparison}

The most important finding is that M4 reaches HDA\,=\,100\% and M2 reaches
83.3\% in customer onboarding -- a result intermediate between procurement
(30.8\%) and incident management (100\%).
This confirms the \emph{signal explicitness gradient} hypothesis with three
data points: HDA increases monotonically as domain risk signals become more
explicit and quantifiable.

           explicit.
           Incident management uses fully explicit binary flags
           (\texttt{datos\_en\_riesgo}, \texttt{sistema\_critico}).

  \label{fig:onb_gradient}

The onboarding case also reveals an important asymmetry between M2 and M4 on
HDA: M2 detects 5 of 6 HiL cases (83.3\%) while M4 detects all 6 (100\%).
The single case missed by M2 (C17: PEP detection) involves a risk factor that
requires cross-cutting synthesis -- the PEP flag combined with extraordinarily
high volume and lack of corporate justification -- exactly the pattern that the
multi-agent architecture with a powerful decision model handles better.

ARR is lower in onboarding (70--75\%) compared to procurement (76--100\%)
because the onboarding decision space is more granular: many cases require
conditional approvals, documentation requests, or credit guarantees rather
than binary approve/reject decisions.
The DER of 40--45\% reflects this complexity -- it is not random error but
systematic under-specification of decision nuances, a known limitation of
the current architecture.

  \label{fig:onb_crossprocess}
\end{figure}

The three-experiment comparison consolidates the central finding of this paper:
neither the single-agent nor the multi-agent architecture is universally
superior -- both depend on the clarity of domain risk signals for HDA, and on
model capacity for ARR.
With explicit signals and a sufficiently powerful decision model, HDA\,=\,100\%
is achievable across different business process types.

of a signal does not guarantee its detection if the model lacks the semantic
capacity to interpret it correctly.

\section{Agent System Prompts}
\label{app:prompts}

The following system prompts were used for each agent.
These are provided as required by Q1 venue standards for reproducibility.

\paragraph{Requester Agent.}
\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
role: "Gestor de Solicitudes"
goal: "Registrar y estructurar solicitudes de compra."
backstory: "Eres el primer punto de contacto. Registras
           solicitudes y las preparas para el equipo."
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Procurement Agent.}
\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
role: "Especialista en Proveedores"
goal: "Validar proveedores y buscar alternativas."
backstory: "Proveedor: estado=X, opera=Y"
           [API catalogue result]
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Finance Agent.}
\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
role: "Analista Financiero"
goal: "Verificar presupuesto y aprobar o proponer reasignacion."
backstory: "Presupuesto: disponible=X EUR, aprobado=Y"
           [budget API result]
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Legal Agent.}
\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
role: "Asesor Legal"
goal: "Revisar clausulas contractuales y detectar riesgos."
backstory: "Legal: riesgo=X, firmar=Y"
           [contract validation API result]
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Compliance Agent (decision-maker, M3/M5).}
\begin{lstlisting}[language={}]
role: "Auditor de Compliance"
goal: "Verificar normativa y decidir."
backstory: "Normativa: cumple=X, riesgo=Y.
            [M5: ESCALA si conflicto interes, GDPR,
             importe >50k EUR, riesgo critico]"
output: JSON {decision, razon, escala_hil}
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Reproducibility Parameters}
\label{app:repro}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Primary model: \texttt{groq/llama-3.1-8b-instant}
  \item Decision model M4/M4b: \texttt{groq/llama-3.3-70b-versatile}
  \item Temperature: 0.0 (initial verification: 5/5 cases identical in single
        session: C01, C21, C39, C43, C50)
  \item Temperature note: single-session verification does not capture
        inter-session variability.
        The statistical robustness analysis (Section~\ref{sec:setup}) reveals
        $\sigma$\,ARR\,=\,11.3\,pp for \texttt{llama-3.1-8b-instant} and
        $\sigma$\,ARR\,=\,0.9\,pp for \texttt{llama-3.3-70b-versatile}.
        \texttt{llama-3.3-70b} is recommended for production environments.
  \item Inter-case pause: M1--M2x: 4\,s; M3--M5: 5--8\,s; M4b: 8\,s
  \item Maximum retries per case: 3
  \item Total LLM calls (single run): M1=0; M2=50; M2x=50; M3=250; M3b=150;
        M4=250; M4b=250; M5=250; M5b=100
  \item Total LLM calls (statistical analysis): M1$\times$3=0;
        M2$\times$3=150; M4$\times$3=750 additional replica calls
  \item Variants executed: 9 models $\times$ 50 cases\,=\,450 total cases
        + 200 cases in statistical replicas
  \item Complete code: \url{https://github.com/Edurne0781/multiagent-procurement-llm}
        (Jupyter Lab, Python 3.12)
\end{itemize}
